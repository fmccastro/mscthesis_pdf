\chapter{Proposed Approach}
\label{chapter:proposedApproach}

This chapter presents some guidelines to approach the solution of the problem formulated in section \ref{section:problemFormulation}. Section \ref{section:approachOptimalPathPlanning} denotes the kind of maps used to generate an optimal path planning method. Sections \ref{section:robotkinematics} and \ref{section:robotDynamics} describe the kinematics and dynamics models used to describe the mobile robot, as well as its derivation. \ref{section:fullStateSpace} describes the selection of \acrshort{nmpc} formulation and steering mechanism, but also presents a standard model-based formulation of the problem. Finally, section \ref{section:modelValidation} validates the model's integrator.

\section{Problem formulation}
\label{section:problemFormulation}

This thesis addresses the problem of developing a nonlinear model predictive control (NMPC) \cite{Rawlings2013, grunenmpc} algorithm, which models the kinematics and dynamics of a rigid wheeled, autonomous robot $\mathcal{R}$ as well as its interactions with the terrain during the traversal of a known, off-the-road, uneven terrain map $\mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^3$. It is required that the vehicle avoids untraversable areas or obstacles $\mathcal{\ObstacleSpace} \subset \mathcal{\Map}$ and tracks a traversable pathway, starting from a given initial admissable pose $\mathcal{R}_{\mathbf{p}_2} \subset \SafeSpace$, where $\mathcal{R}_{\mathbf{p}_2}$ denotes the robot circled hull around $\mathbf{p}_2$ and $\SafeSpace$ is a safe space, such that $\SafeSpace \cap \ObstacleSpace = \emptyset$. A minimum safety distance to the nearest obstacle shall be ensured so that $\underset{\mathbf{p}_2 \in \SafeSpace,\, \mathbf{o} \in \ObstacleSpace}{\text{min}}\; d(\mathbf{p}_2, \mathbf{o}) > \underline{d}$. Moreover, the robot $\mathcal{R}$ shall make a fast, safe and slippage-free traverse.  The NMPC algorithm must deliver optimal solutions and meet stringent real-time requirements.

\section{Optimal path planning}
\label{section:approachOptimalPathPlanning}

Although this thesis is mainly focused on nonlinear model predictive control applications to rough terrain, optimal path planning methods for this kind of terrain are also surveyed.

\begin{assumption}
	The elevation map is assumed to be known \ti{a-priori} without uncertainties.
	\label{assumption:elevationmap}
\end{assumption}

Due to its compactness and simplicity, digital elevation maps (DEM) are chosen to describe the elevation $\z_{ij}$ at each location $(\x_i,\, \y_j)$. Then, from the compacted data, a cost map is generated in order to yield a traversability map  of the environment, by assigining a traversability cost $\TraversabilityCost_{ij}$ to each grid point $(\x_i,\, \y_j)$. This choice stems from the literature review presented in subsection \ref{subsection:pathPlanning}, but also because it is easily combined with a minimum-cost planner algorithm, in order to output a global planner. These maps are considered to share the same frame.

Then, some method shall be found to process the DEM information against the mobile robot kinematic features (the dynamics features were neglected in this analysis). At the end, the terrain roughness and inclination shall be evaluated. From the traversability map that comes from this algorithm, an optimal path shall be dgenerated.

To meet that end, an optimal path planner based on the fast marching method is developed to solve this problem, as it delivers a potential field with an unique minimum point at the goal. The work of \citet{Amorim2014} sheds some light on this, whereas \citet{Ishigami2007PathPlanning} provides some guidance regarding the roughness map computation.

\section{Mobile robot kinematics}
\label{section:robotkinematics}

Kinematics describe the motion geometry of a point or body according to the provided actuations. A four-wheeled rigid vehicle kinematics model is derived in this chapter, which can be applied both to skid-steering or differentially driven mobile robots.

A wheeled mobile robot $\Robot$ is moving on a map $\Map$, which is centered at the origin of an inertial frame $\WorldFrame$. A moving frame $\BodyFrame$ is centered on the robot's center of mass \acrshort{com} and it is fixed to $\Robot$. Although the mobile robot operates on unstructured, uneven, off-the-road terrains, not all degrees of freedom are controllable. This issue is addressed in the following sections.

\begin{assumption}
	In moving frame $\BodyFrame$, $\BodyFrame_x$ points forward, $\BodyFrame_y$ points to the left and $\BodyFrame_z$ points upwards.
	\label{assumption:frames}
\end{assumption}

The mobile robot position and orientation with respect to frame $\WorldFrame$ is described as $\mathbf{\Position}_3 = [x\;  y\; z]^T$ and $\bm{\Orientation} = [\roll \; \pitch \; \yaw]^T$, respectively. On the other hand, the mobile robot linear and angular velocities with respect to frame $\BodyFrame$ stands as $\mathbf{\LinVelocity} = [ \LinVelocity_x\;\LinVelocity_y\;\LinVelocity_z ]^T$ and $\bm{\AngVelocity} = [\AngVelocity_x\;\AngVelocity_y\;\AngVelocity_z]^T$, respectively. Then, it is possible to map the mobile robot $\Robot$ linear velocity between frames $\BodyFrame$ and $\WorldFrame$ by taking advantage of rotation matrix \eqref{eq:rotationMatrixW2B}, but also to map euler angle rates in $\WorldFrame$ to angular velocities in $\BodyFrame$ with rotation matrix \eqref{eq:rotationMatrixW2BRotationalInverse}.

\begin{align}
	\DerMathBold{\Position}_3 = \mathbf{\RotMatrix}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame} \mathbf{\LinVelocity} \label{eq:translationFreeBody}\\
	\DerMathBold{\Orientation} = \mathbf{\bar{\RotMatrix}}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame} \bm{\AngVelocity} \label{eq:rotationFreeBody}
\end{align}

\subsection*{Wheel kinematics}
\label{subsection:wheelKinematics}

A kinematics model is not only described by its free-body motion equations \eqref{eq:translationFreeBody} and \eqref{eq:rotationFreeBody}, but also by geometrical constraints \cite{Kozlowski2004}. Each wheel is actuated by a torque $\Torque_i$, which induces on it a spin rate, $\AngVelocity_i,\, i = 1,\,...,\,N_w$, where $N_w = 4$ for the cases presented in this thesis.

\begin{assumption}
	The thickness and deformation of each wheel are neglected so that the wheel is assumed to be rigid and to contact the terrain at a single contact point $\contactPoint_i$, in case rigid terrain is the working environment \cite{Iagnemma2004}.
	\label{assumption:singlePointContact}
\end{assumption}

Due to the fact that wheels are fixed, the mobile robot holds two controllable degrees of freedom: translation along its longitudinal direction $\BodyFrame_x$, and rotation around its vertical axis $\BodyFrame_z$. Therefore, lateral skidding from the vehicle is expected to remain close to zero on flat terrain, although its wheels are prone to skidding when the mobile robot finds itself on uneven or inclined surfaces (figure \ref{fig:wheelForcesMoments}). Moreover, the velocity vector of each wheel is tangent to the terrain that each wheel is touching on.

\section{Mobile robot dynamics}
\label{section:robotDynamics}

Each wheel joint can only be actuated around the wheel horizontal axis, which is parallel to $\WheelFrame_y^i$. Then, it also holds a contact frame $\WheelFrame^i$ which follows up the terrain slope. Apart from it, each wheel is subject to forces and moments coming from its interaction with the terrain or due to gravity (figure \ref{fig:wheelForcesMoments}). Finally, these forces and moments shall be expressed with respect to frame $\BodyFrame$ and summed up in order to feed the dynamics relationship of the mobile robot, which yields \refeq{eq:dynamics}.

The weight distribution on the mobile robot wheels is influenced by its roll and pitch. A dynamics model is derived based on Newton's second law. The angular momentum rate of change of the whole mobile robot around its COM is also considered.

\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\Mass \mathbf{\dot{\LinVelocity}} + \Mass \; \bm{\AngVelocity} \times \mathbf{\LinVelocity} &= \mathbf{\Force} \\
		\mathbf{\InertiaTensor} \bm{ \dot{\AngVelocity} } + \bm{\AngVelocity} \times (\mathbf{\InertiaTensor} \bm{\AngVelocity}) &= \mathbf{\Moment}
	\end{aligned}	
	\label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}

\noindent , where $\Mass$ stands for the mobile robot mass. Moreover, $\mathbf{\InertiaTensor}$ denotes the inertia tensor of the mobile robot with respect to frame $\BodyFrame$. Furthermore, $\mathbf{\Force} = [\Force_x\;\Force_y\;\Force_z]^T$ stands for the sum of forces acting on the system and $\mathbf{\Moment} = [\Moment_x \; \Moment_y \; \Moment_z]^T$ denotes the sum of moments. $\mathbf{\Force}$ and $\mathbf{\Moment}$ are expressed with respect to frame $\BodyFrame$. 

Equation \eqref{eq:dynamics} may be obtained by applying the second newton law for translational and rotational dynamics. It is of interest to find out a dynamics formulation which is expressed with respect to $\BodyFrame$, because $\mathbf{\InertiaTensor}$ is time invariant there, but also because it is more intuitive to think of velocities, forces and moments with respect to $\BodyFrame$. In case the linear and angular moments are defined by $\mathbf{\linearMoment} = \Mass \mathbf{\LinVelocity}$ and $\mathbf{\angularMoment} = \mathbf{\InertiaTensor} \bm{\AngVelocity}$ respectively, it is possible to derive a dynamics formulation by applying \refeq{eq:vectorDiff2} with respect to inertial frame $\WorldFrame$ and non-inertial frame $\BodyFrame$.

\subsection{Wheel actuation}
\label{subsection:wheelActuation}

Every wheel is commanded by a torque, $\bm{\Torque}_i$. That torque yields a traction force $\mathbf{\Traction}_i$ which is expressed with respect to wheel frame $\WheelFrame^i$ (figure \ref{fig:wheelForcesMoments}). The traction force $\mathbf{\Traction}_i$ only holds a single longitudinal component within $\WheelFrame^i$. $\mathbf{\Traction}_i$ is the result of the action of the soil on the wheel at $\contactPoint_i$. Therefore, the force produced by $\bm{\Torque}_i$ on the soil opposes $\mathbf{\Traction}_i$.

\begin{assumption}
In this thesis, it is considered that every wheel lies on the same plane at a given time. Then, every wheel contact frame $\WheelFrame^i$ is parallel to $\BodyFrame$.
\label{assumption:contactbodyframes}
\end{assumption}

The consequence of assumption \ref{assumption:contactbodyframes} is that every traction force $\mathbf{\Traction}_i$ is already expressed with respect to $\BodyFrame$. Then, the sum of traction forces, $\mathbf{\Traction}$, with respect to $\BodyFrame$ is simply expressed by,

\begin{align}
	\mathbf{\Traction} &= \sum_{i} \mathbf{\Traction}_i \label{eq:totalActuatedForce}\\
	\mathbf{\Moment}_{\mathbf{\Traction}} &= \sum_{i} \mathbf{\contactPoint}_i \times \mathbf{\Traction}_i
		\label{eq:totalActuatedMoment}	
\end{align}

\noindent, where $\mathbf{\contactPoint}_i$ is a vector connecting the mobile robot \acrshort{com} to the contact point $\contactPoint_i$, with respect to $\BodyFrame$, and $i = \{lf,\,rf,\,lb,\,rb\}$ denote the set of wheels of the mobile robot. The traction forces generated by wheel torque actuation may also output a moment around \acrshort{com} with respect to $\BodyFrame$, denoted by $\mathbf{\Moment}_{\mathbf{\Traction}}$.

\subsubsection*{Lateral wheel traction force}

The lateral wheel traction force is considered to counter act the respective weight component. Therefore, $\mathbf{\Traction}$ solely has a longitudinal and lateral component, such that $\mathbf{\Traction}_i = \left[\Traction_x \;\; \frac{-\Mass\,\gravity\,\cos(\pitch) \sin(\roll)}{4} \;\; 0\right]^T_i$. The lateral traction force also behaves as a reaction force, thus it is not controllable.

\begin{assumption}
The wheel lateral traction is considered to cancel the weight lateral component, therefore $\left(\Traction_y\right)_i = \frac{-\Mass\,\gravity\,\cos(\pitch) \sin(\roll)}{4}$.
\label{assumption:wheellateraltraction}
\end{assumption}

\subsection{Wheel-terrain contact model}
\label{subsection:wheelTerrainContactModel}

The ability to accurately model the interaction forces and moments between a wheel and the terrain is of utmost importance, in order to simulate a theoretical model of a robot or to control a real-life application at a given environment.

There are four possible cases of wheel-terrain interaction (figure \ref{fig:contactModels}): rigid wheel on rigid terrain, rigid wheel on deformable terrain, deformable wheel on rigid terrain and a deformable wheel on deformable terrain. In \cite{Genta2012, chapter24handbookrobotics}, one can find analytical models for all these possibilities.

\end{figure}

In this thesis, only rigid wheels are considered. Even if a wheel presents some degree of deformability (i.e. pneumatic tire), it can be modeled as a rigid wheel if the average tire ground pressure is higher than the critical ground pressure, $p_{gcr}$ \cite{chapter24handbookrobotics}.

When it comes to the type of soil, a rover may traverse deformable or rigid terrain when it is deployed at an outdoor, natural environment, so it is difficult to predict \textit{a priori} what kind of soil it will be traversing as well as its properties.

The theory of terramechanics focuses on studying the mechanical properties of soils and to assess its behaviour, when it is subject to deformation caused on it by an external agent with a locomotion set. Apart from it, terramechanics models also deliver performance quantification by describing the stress fields that the contact modules are exposed to. In case of deformable soils, the standard model descriptor is the Bekker-Wong model. On the other hand, the Coulomb friction cone is a simple model that describes the contact dynamics between rigid wheels and rigid terrain.

For the sake of simplicity, deformable terrain contact physics are discarded as a simulation option, although this more realistic terrain may be included in more advanced research works.

\subsubsection*{Coulomb friction model}
\label{subsubsection:coulombFrictionModel}

This model provides a traction force, $\mathbf{\Traction}$, acting at each wheel, as a result of the action of the soil on the wheel. $\mathbf{\Traction}$ breaks into a longitudinal ($\Traction_x$) and lateral ($\Traction_y$) component, in case $\mathbf{\Traction}$ is expressed with respect to $\WheelFrame^i$. Apart from this, a terrain reaction force $\mathbf{n}$, acts at $\contactPoint_i$, with a direction parallel to the contact normal at $\contactPoint_i$. Due to assumption \ref{assumption:contactbodyframes}, $\mathbf{n}$ only holds a single vertical component within $\WheelFrame^i$. The Coulomb friction model \cite{chapter24handbookrobotics} states the following,

\begin{equation}
	\begin{cases}
		\left(\frac{\Traction_x}{\FrictionCoefficient_x \NormalForce_z} \right)_i^2 + \left( \frac{\Traction_y}{\FrictionCoefficient_y \NormalForce_z} \right)_i^2 \leq 1\\
		\left( \NormalForce_z \right)_i > 0
	\end{cases}
	\label{eq:coulombFrictionModel}
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:coulombFrictionModel} states that $\mathbf{\Traction}_i$ lies within an ellipse with diagonals $\FrictionCoefficient_x\,\left( \NormalForce_z \right)_i$ and $\FrictionCoefficient_y\,\left( \NormalForce_z \right)_i$. In case $\FrictionCoefficient_x = \FrictionCoefficient_y$, the ellipse turns into a circle. $\FrictionCoefficient_x$ and $\FrictionCoefficient_y$ denote friction coefficients along the longitudinal and lateral components of $\WheelFrame^i$, respectively.

\begin{assumption}
$\FrictionCoefficient_x = \FrictionCoefficient_y$.
\label{eq:sameFrictionCoefficients}
\end{assumption}

From assumption \ref{eq:sameFrictionCoefficients} and if one states that $\Traction_i^2 = \left( \Traction_x \right)_i^2 + \left( \Traction_y \right)_i^2$, it comes that,

\begin{equation}
\Traction_i^2 \leq \left( \FrictionCoefficient \left( \NormalForce _z\right)_i \right)^2
\label{eq:simpleFriction}
\end{equation}

If a wheel $i$ sticks to the terrain, it means \refeq{eq:simpleFriction} is generally inactive, unless the contact force touches the cone lateral surface. On the other hand, if a wheel is sliding, inequality \eqref{eq:simpleFriction} is not respected. Figure \ref{fig:coulombfrictionconeillustration} depicts this principle.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/coulomb_friction_cone.pdf}
  \caption[Coulomb friction cone depiction example.]{Coulomb friction cone depiction. If the wheel contact force ($\mathbf{n} + \mathbf{t}$) is \textcolor{tabred}{outside} the cone, the wheel is slipping. On the other hand, if the wheel contact force is \textcolor{tabgreen}{inside} the cone (including its surface), the wheel sticks to the terrain. The right picture presents a top view of the left picture.}
  \label{fig:coulombfrictionconeillustration}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection*{Terrain reaction forces}
\label{subsubsection:coulombFrictionModel}

Every time a contact between a wheel and terrain takes place, a reaction force $\NormalForce_z$ shows up. The magnitude of this reaction force determines the amount of admissable traction force, before soil failure occurs. These reaction forces and respective moments shall be summed up and described with respect to $\BodyFrame$, as it is oulined in equations \eqref{eq:totalReactionForce} and \eqref{eq:totalReactionMoments},

\begin{align}
	\mathbf{\NormalForce} = \sum_{i} \left( \mathbf{\NormalForce} \right)_i
	\label{eq:totalReactionForce}\\
	\mathbf{\Moment}_{\mathbf{\NormalForce}} = \sum_{i} \mathbf{\contactPoint}_i \times \mathbf{\NormalForce}_i
	\label{eq:totalReactionMoments}	
\end{align}

\noindent , where $\left( \mathbf{\NormalForce} \right)_i = \left[0 \;\; 0 \;\; \frac{-\Mass\,\gravity\,\cos(\pitch) \cos(\roll)}{4} \right]^T_i$.

\hfill
\begin{assumption}
Assumption \ref{assumption:contactbodyframes} determines that the direction of each wheel reaction force is parallel to $\BodyFrame_z$ and cancels the weight vertical component at each wheel. For the sake of simplicity, it is also considered that the magnitude of each wheel reaction force is equal to $\frac{\Mass\,\gravity\,\cos(\pitch) \cos(\roll)}{4}$, therefore $\mathbf{\Moment}_{\mathbf{\NormalForce}} = 0$.
\label{eq:reactionforcessimplification}
\end{assumption}

\section{NMPC formulation}
\label{section:fullStateSpace}

In order to sum up the previous sections, it is possible to deliver a full state-space model with respect to the state vector $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{\Position}^T & \bm{\Orientation}^T & \mathbf{\LinVelocity}^T & \bm{\AngVelocity}^T
\end{bmatrix}$ and the control vector $\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{\Force}^T & \mathbf{\Moment}^T
\end{bmatrix}$. Nonlinear constraints to the model are also included.

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{f}_{skid}\left( t, \,\mathbf{x}(t),\, \mathbf{u}(t) \right) = \left\{\begin{matrix}
		\DerMathBold{\Position} = \mathbf{\RotMatrix}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame} \mathbf{\LinVelocity} & \DerBold{\Orientation} = \mathbf{\bar{\RotMatrix}}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame} \bm{\AngVelocity} \\
		\mathbf{\dot{\LinVelocity}} = -\bm{\AngVelocity} \times \mathbf{\LinVelocity} + \frac{1}{\Mass} \mathbf{\Force} & \bm{ \dot{\AngVelocity} } = \mathbf{\InertiaTensor}^{-1} \left( -\bm{\AngVelocity} \times \left( \mathbf{\InertiaTensor} \bm{\AngVelocity} \right) + \mathbf{\Moment}\right)
	\end{matrix}\right.
	\label{eq:fullStateSpace}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
	\begin{matrix} \mathbf{\Force} = \mathbf{n} + \mathbf{\Traction} + \mathbf{g} & \mathbf{\Moment} = \mathbf{\Moment_\NormalForceSubscript} + \mathbf{\Moment}_{\mathbf{\Traction}} & \mathbf{g} = \mathbf{\RotMatrix}_{\WorldFrame}^{\BodyFrame} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & m\,g\end{bmatrix}^T \end{matrix}
	\label{eq:sumMomentsForces}
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:fullStateSpace} combines equations \eqref{eq:translationFreeBody}, \eqref{eq:rotationFreeBody} and \eqref{eq:dynamics}. Equation \eqref{eq:sumMomentsForces} is based on equations \eqref{eq:totalActuatedForce}, \eqref{eq:totalActuatedMoment},
\eqref{eq:totalReactionForce} and \eqref{eq:totalReactionMoments}. $m$ stands for the vehicle mass and $g=-9.81 m/s^2$.

\begin{equation}
	\ineqCon{t, \,\mathbf{x}(t),\, \mathbf{u}(t)} = \begin{cases}
		t_i^2 - \left( \FrictionCoefficient \left(n_z\right)_i \right)^2 \leq 0\\
		\left(n_z\right)_i > 0
	\end{cases}, \; i = 1, ..., 4
	\label{eq:inequalityConstraintsModel}
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:inequalityConstraintsModel} denotes the inequality constraints imposed by the Coulomb friction model.

\subsection{Model simplification}

In this thesis, skid-steering is selected as steering mechanism. Likewise, \refeq{eq:sumForces} expresses the sum of forces and moments due to traction,

\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
\Force_x &= \Traction_l + \Traction_r\\
\Moment_z &= \frac{\left( \Traction_r - \Traction_l \right) \, l}{2}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:sumForces}
\end{equation}

\noindent, where $l$ stands for the distance between the left and right wheels, respectively. In this mechanism, lateral skidding only takes place in case the non-controllable lateral traction forces cannot balance the lateral weight, so $\LinVelocity_y \approx 0$.

\begin{assumption}
The consequence of assumption \ref{assumption:wheellateraltraction} is that $\LinVelocity_y \approx 0$.
\label{eq:lateralskid}
\end{assumption}

On top of that, the dynamics formulation of the models assume that the vehicle moves on the same plane along the control horizon, which yields the following assumption.

\begin{assumption}
As the wheeled mobile robot is constrained to move on a plane along the \acrshort{nmpc} horizon, it comes that $\LinVelocity_z \approx 0,\, \AngVelocity_x \approx 0,\,\AngVelocity_y \approx 0$. The roll and pitch angles are continuosly updated, between consecutive iterations of the problem.
\label{eq:planarmotion}
\end{assumption}

The model fails once the mobile robot finds itself in a scenario where the lateral wheel traction achieves its maximum and cannot equal the weight lateral component (assumption \ref{eq:lateralskid} is not valid anymore in that case). In order to stay away from that scenario, the mobile robot shall avoid very high slopes.

According to assumptions \ref{eq:lateralskid} and \ref{eq:planarmotion}, the state space \refeq{eq:fullStateSpace} is reduced to the one in \eqref{eq:skidsteeringStateSpace}. Likewise, the constraint function \eqref{eq:inequalityConstraintsModel} is simplified to the one of \eqref{eq:skidsteeringcoulomb}, where the Coulomb friction model is included, alongside linear and angular velocity constraints.

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{f}_{skid}\left( t, \,\mathbf{x}(t),\, \mathbf{u}(t) \right) = \left\{\begin{matrix}
		\dot{x} = \cos\left(\pitch\right) \cos\left(\yaw\right) \LinVelocity_x & \dot{y} = \cos\left(\pitch\right) \sin\left(\yaw\right)\LinVelocity_x & \dot{\roll} = -\tan\left(\pitch\right)\cos\left(\roll\right)\,\AngVelocity_z\\
		\dot{\pitch} = \sin\left(\roll\right)\,\AngVelocity_z &
		\dot{\yaw} = \frac{\cos\left(\roll\right)}{\cos\left(\pitch\right)}\,\AngVelocity_z &
		\dot{\LinVelocity}_x = \frac{\Force_x}{\Mass} - \sin\left(\pitch\right)\,\gravity\\
		\dot{\AngVelocity}_z = \frac{\Moment_z}{\InertiaTensor_{zz}} &
		\dot{\Traction}_l = u_l &
		\dot{\Traction}_r = u_r
	\end{matrix}\right.
	\label{eq:skidsteeringStateSpace}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\ineqCon{ t,\,\mathbf{x}(t),\,\mathbf{u}(t) } = \begin{cases}
\Traction_{l,\,r}^2 + \left(-\frac{ \Mass \, \gravity \, \cos\left(\pitch\right) \, \sin\left(\roll\right)}{4}\right)^2 \leq \left(\FrictionCoefficient\,\frac{ \Mass \, \gravity \, \cos\left(\roll\right) \, \cos\left(\pitch\right)}{4}\right)^2\\
\left(\LinVelocity_x\right)_{min} \leq \LinVelocity_x \leq \left(\LinVelocity_x\right)_{max}\\
\left(\AngVelocity_z\right)_{min} \leq \AngVelocity_z \leq \left(\AngVelocity_z\right)_{max}\\
\end{cases}
\label{eq:skidsteeringcoulomb}
\end{equation}

In \refeq{eq:skidsteeringStateSpace}, $\Force_x$ and $\Moment_z$ are derived from \refeq{eq:sumForces}. The control and state vectors are $\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix}u_l \;\; u_r\end{bmatrix}^T$ and $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}\x \;\; \y \;\; \roll \;\; \pitch \;\; \yaw \;\; \LinVelocity_x \;\;\AngVelocity_z \;\; \Traction_l \;\; \Traction_r\end{bmatrix}^T$, respectively. Finally, $\mathbf{p} = \begin{bmatrix}
\Mass \;\; \gravity \;\; \InertiaTensor_{zz} \;\; \FrictionCoefficient\end{bmatrix}^T$ denote the parameters of the model.

\subsection{Maximum inclination}
\label{subsection:maxinclination}

As it was outlined in subsection \ref{subsection:wheelTerrainContactModel}, the wheel normal force and the terrain friction coefficient restrict the amount of traction force the wheel can transfer to the ground. Apart from it, the non-controllable wheel lateral force also limits the controllable longitudinal wheel traction force.

\begin{equation}
\underbrace{ \tan^2\left(\pitch\right) }_{ f_1\left(\pitch\right) } \leq \underbrace{ \FrictionCoefficient^2\,\cos^2\left(\roll\right) - \sin^2\left(\roll\right) }_{ f_2\left(\mu,\;\roll\right) }
\label{eq:ForcesRest}
\end{equation}

Due to that, the traversal of high slopes pose a great risk to the mobile robot's safety. If the set $\left(\roll,\, \pitch,\,\FrictionCoefficient\right)$ is close to or at the activation of \refeq{eq:ForcesRest} when the mobile robot is at steady-state, slippage will likely come up, regardless of the controller's attempt to avoid it, so these situations shall be avoided (see chapter \ref{chapter:globalpathplanner}). Equation \eqref{eq:ForcesRest} is obtained by replacing $\Traction_{l,\,r}$ on \refeq{eq:skidsteeringcoulomb} by $\sin\left(\pitch\right)\,\frac{\Mass\,\gravity}{4}$.
