\section{Euler angles}
\label{section:eulerAngles}

Euler rotation angles defining an extrinsic $x-y-z$ (roll-pitch-yaw) rotation, with a rotation sequence of $x-y-z$ around the inertial frame $\WorldFrame$ is denoted as,

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{\RotMatrix}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame} = \bm{\RotMatrix}_z (\yaw) \bm{\RotMatrix}_y (\pitch) \bm{\RotMatrix}_x (\roll)
\end{equation}

, with the previous multiplication to yield a mapping from frame $\BodyFrame$ to $\WorldFrame$, which stands as,
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
	\mathbf{\RotMatrix}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame} (\roll, \pitch, \yaw) &=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\cos(\yaw) & -\sin(\yaw) & 0 \\
		\sin(\yaw) & \cos(\yaw) & 0 \\
		0 & 0 & 1
	\end{bmatrix}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\cos(\pitch) & 0 & \sin(\pitch) \\
		0 & 1 & 0 \\
		-\sin(\pitch) & 0 & \cos(\pitch)
	\end{bmatrix}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		1 & 0 & 0 \\
		0 & \cos(\roll) & -\sin(\roll) \\
		0 & \sin(\roll) & \cos(\roll)
	\end{bmatrix} \\
	& =
	\begin{bmatrix} 
		\cos(\yaw) \cos(\pitch) & \cos(\yaw) \sin(\pitch) \sin(\phi) - \sin(\yaw) \cos(\phi) & \cos(\yaw) \sin(\pitch) \cos(\roll) + \sin(\yaw) \sin(\roll) \\
		\sin(\yaw) \cos(\pitch) & \sin(\yaw) \sin(\pitch) \sin(\roll) + \cos(\yaw) \cos(\roll) & \sin(\yaw) \sin(\pitch) \cos(\roll) - \cos(\yaw) \sin(\roll) \\
		-\sin(\pitch) & \cos(\pitch) \sin(\roll) & \cos(\pitch) \cos(\roll)
	\end{bmatrix}
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:rotationMatrixW2B}
\end{equation}

Rotation matrices are singular ($\det(\bm{\RotMatrix}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame}) = 1$) and symmetric, $(\mathbf{\RotMatrix}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame})^T = (\mathbf{\RotMatrix}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame})^{-1} = \mathbf{\RotMatrix}_{\WorldFrame}^{\BodyFrame}$. Euler rotation matrices can be derived from 24 different conventions ranging from extrinsic or intrinsic rotation with different rotation orders. More information on this can be found in \cite{Roithmayr2015}.

The mapping between the euler angle rates and the body-frame angular velocities can be found by transforming the euler rates from the intermediate frames to body frame $\BodyFrame$.

\begin{equation}
	\bm{\AngVelocity} = \begin{bmatrix}
	0 \\ 0 \\ \dot{\yaw}
	\end{bmatrix} + \mathbf{R}_z(\yaw) \begin{bmatrix}
	0 \\ \dot{\pitch} \\ 0
	\end{bmatrix} + \mathbf{R}_z(\yaw) \mathbf{R}_y(\pitch) \begin{bmatrix}
	\dot{\roll} \\ 0 \\ 0
	\end{bmatrix} =	\begin{bmatrix}
		cos(\yaw) \cdot cos(\pitch) & -sin(\yaw) & 0\\
		sin(\yaw) \cdot cos(\pitch) & cos(\yaw) & 0\\
		-sin(\pitch) & 0 & 1
	\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
	\dot{\roll} \\ \dot{\pitch} \\ \dot{\yaw}
	\end{bmatrix} 
	\label{eq:rotationMatrixW2BRotational}
\end{equation}

Then, the inverse of the matrix presented in \ref{eq:rotationMatrixW2BRotational} denotes the mapping from the angular velocities with respect to $\BodyFrame$, to the euler rates with respect to $\WorldFrame$. That matrix is denoted in equation \ref{eq:rotationMatrixW2BRotationalInverse} and it is denoted in this document by $\mathbf{\bar{R}}_{\BodyFrame}^{\WorldFrame}$.

\begin{equation}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\dot{\roll} \\ \dot{\pitch} \\ \dot{\yaw}
	\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
		\frac{cos(\yaw)}{cos(\pitch)} & \frac{sin(\yaw)}{cos(\pitch)} & 0 \\
		-sin(\yaw) & cos(\yaw) & 0 \\
		cos(\yaw) \cdot \tan(\pitch) & tan(\pitch) \cdot sin(\yaw) & 1
	\end{bmatrix} \bm{\AngVelocity}
	\label{eq:rotationMatrixW2BRotationalInverse}
\end{equation}

\section{Skew-symmetric matrix}
\label{section:skewSymmetricMatrix}

A skew-symmetric matrix $\bm{S}$ is a square matrix whose transpose equals its negative, $\bm{S}^T = -\bm{S}$. In this thesis, three by three matrices are employed which take the following form,
\begin{equation}
	\bm{S(r} = (x, \; y, \; z)\bm{)} = \begin{bmatrix}
								0 & -z & y\\
								z & 0 & -x\\
								-y & x & 0	
	\end{bmatrix}
	\label{eq:skewSymmetricMatrix} 
\end{equation}

In case this matrix is multiplied by a column vector $\mathbf{a}$, it is the same as applying the cross product between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{a}$. Then, it is stated that,
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{S(r)} \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{a}
	\label{eq:skewSymmetricMatrixProperties} 
\end{equation}

\section{Inertia tensor}
\label{section:inertiaTensor}

The inertia tensor of a rigid body is given by,
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{\InertiaTensor} = \begin{bmatrix}
		\InertiaTensor_{xx} & \InertiaTensor_{xy} & \InertiaTensor_{xz}\\
		\InertiaTensor_{yx} & \InertiaTensor_{yy} & \InertiaTensor_{yz}\\
		\InertiaTensor_{zx} & \InertiaTensor_{zy} & \InertiaTensor_{zz}
	\end{bmatrix}
	\label{eq:annexInertiaTensor}
\end{equation}

, where the products of inertia are equal between each other ($I_{xy} = I_{yx}$, $I_{xz} = I_{zx}$, $I_{yz} = I_{zy}$).

\section{Differentiation of a vector}
\label{section:vectorDiff}

If a vector is expressed with respect to a non-inertial frame $\mathcal{P}$, it is still possible to differentiate that same vector with respect to an inertial frame $\mathcal{Q}$ \cite{Beer2015}.

Let $\mathbf{p} = p_1 \; \mathbf{i} + p_2 \; \mathbf{j} + p_3 \; \mathbf{k}$, with $\mathbf{i}, \; \mathbf{j}, \; \mathbf{k}$ to be the canonical vectors of a non-inertial frame $\mathcal{P}$. Then, the differentiation of $\mathbf{p}$ with respect to $\mathcal{Q}$ is given by,
\begin{equation}
	\derivative{\mathbf{p}}\Bigr|_{\mathcal{Q}} = \dot{p}_1 \; \mathbf{i} + \dot{p}_2 \; \mathbf{j} + \dot{p}_3 \; \mathbf{k} + p_1 \derivative{\mathbf{i}} + p_2 \derivative{\mathbf{j}} + p_3 \derivative{\mathbf{k}}
	\label{eq:vectorDiff}
\end{equation}
 
, where $\derivative{\mathbf{p}}\Bigr|_{\mathcal{P}} = \dot{p}_1 \; \mathbf{i} + \dot{p}_2 \; \mathbf{j} + \dot{p}_3 \; \mathbf{k}$ because the canonical vectors $\mathbf{i}, \; \mathbf{j}, \; \mathbf{k}$ do not change with respect to time if the observer is located at the origin of $\mathcal{P}$. According to the general expression $\derivative{\mathbf{r}} = \bm{\Omega} \times \mathbf{r}$, it comes that $\derivative{\mathbf{i}} = \bm{\Omega} \times \mathbf{i}, \; \derivative{\mathbf{j}} = \bm{\Omega} \times \mathbf{j}, \; \derivative{\mathbf{k}} = \bm{\Omega} \times \mathbf{k}$. Then, equation \ref{eq:vectorDiff} may be rewritten as,

\begin{equation}
	\derivative{\mathbf{p}}\Bigr|_{\mathcal{Q}} = \derivative{\mathbf{p}}\Bigr|_{\mathcal{P}} + \mathbf{\Omega} \times \mathbf{p} 
	\label{eq:vectorDiff2}
\end{equation} 

, where $\bm{\Omega}$ denotes the angular velocity of frame $\mathcal{P}$ with respect to frame $\mathcal{Q}$.

\section{Runge-Kutta 4 numerical integrator}
\label{section:rk4}

The Runge-Kutta 4 numerical integrator takes on a continuous \acrshort{ode} \ref{eq:ode_ivp} with an initial value condition such as,
\begin{subequations} \label{eq:ode_ivp}
	\begin{align}
		\DerMathBold{x} &= \processModel{\mathbf{x}(t),\,\mathbf{z}_1(t),\,\ldots,\mathbf{z}_n(t),\,t} \label{subeq:ode_dynamics} \\
		\mathbf{x}(t_0) &= \mathbf{\hat{x}}_0 \label{subeq:ode_initialcond}
	\end{align}
\end{subequations}
and performs a numerical integration on a small interval like $\left[t_0,\,t_1\right]$ where $t_1 = t_0 + h$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{x}(t_1) &= \mathbf{x}(t_0) + \frac{h}{6}\left(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3+ k_4\right)\\
\text{with} \hspace{2mm} k_1 &= f\left( \mathbf{x}(t_0),\,\mathbf{z}_1(t_0),\,\ldots,\mathbf{z}_n(t_0),\,t_0 \right)\\
k_2 &= f\left( \mathbf{x}(t_0) + h \frac{k_1}{2},\,\mathbf{z}_1(t_0),\,\ldots,\mathbf{z}_n(t_0),\,t_0+\frac{h}{2} \right)\\
k_3 &= f\left( \mathbf{x}(t_0) + h \frac{k_2}{2},\,\mathbf{z}_1(t_0),\,\ldots,\mathbf{z}_n(t_0),\,t_0+\frac{h}{2} \right)\\
k_4 &= f\left( \mathbf{x}(t_0) + h k_3,\,\mathbf{z}_1(t_0),\,\ldots,\mathbf{z}_n(t_0),\,t_0+h \right)\\
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:rk4}
\end{equation}

, where $\mathbf{x}(\cdot) \in \mathbb{R}^{n_x}$ is the general state and $\mathbf{z}_1(\cdot) \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{z_1}},\,\ldots,\mathbf{z}_n(\cdot) \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{z_n}}$ are secondary variables and $f:\mathbb{R}^{n_x + n_{z_1} + \ldots+n_{z_n}} \to \mathbb{R}^{n_x}$ is the \acrshort{ode} dynamics.

\subsection{Sensitivities propagation}
\label{subsection:sensitivitiespropagation}

A sensitivity is defined as the rate of change of some variable $x_1$ with respect to another variable $x_2$, $\pdv{x_1}{x_2} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{x_1}\, \times\, n_{x_2}}$. In this thesis, one is interested in evaluating the sensitivities of $\mathbf{x}$ with respect to another variable of the dynamics model $\processModel{\cdot}$ and on propagating these sensitivities \cite{quirynen2015autogenerating}. 

Then, let's define $S_{\mathbf{x}}(t) = \pdv{\mathbf{x}(t)}{\mathbf{x}_0}$ and $S_{\mathbf{z}_i}(t) = \pdv{\mathbf{x}(t)}{\mathbf{z}_i}$. These sensitivities may be propagated forward in time, by deriving its rate of change. So, if one applies $\pdv{}{\mathbf{x}_0}$ and $\pdv{}{\mathbf{z}_i}$  to \ref{subeq:ode_dynamics}, the application of the chain rule yields the following,

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\dot{S}_{\mathbf{x}}(t) &= \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{x}} S_{\mathbf{x}}(t) + \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{z}_1} \cancel{\pdv{\mathbf{z}_1(t)}{\mathbf{x}_0}} + \ldots + \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{z}_n} \cancel{\pdv{\mathbf{z}_n(t)}{\mathbf{x}_0}} = \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{x}} S_{\mathbf{x}}(t)\\
\dot{S}_{\mathbf{z}_i}(t) &= \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{x}} S_{\mathbf{z}_i}(t) + \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{z}_1} \cancel{\pdv{\mathbf{z}_1(t)}{\mathbf{z}_i}} + \ldots + \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{z}_i} \cancelto{1}{\pdv{\mathbf{z}_i(t)}{\mathbf{z}_i}} + \ldots + \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{z}_n} \cancel{\pdv{\mathbf{z}_n(t)}{\mathbf{z}_i}}\\
&= \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{x}} S_{\mathbf{z}_i}(t) + \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}}{\mathbf{z}_i},\; i = \{1,\,\ldots,n\}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:sensitivitiespropagation}
\end{equation}

, where $\processModel{\cdot}$ denotes $\processModel{\mathbf{x}(t),\,\mathbf{z}_1(t),\,\ldots,\mathbf{z}_n(t),\,t}$ for the sake of simplicity.

\section{Parallel-axis theorem}
\label{section:parallelAxisTheorem}

The parallel-axis theorem \cite{Beer2015} denotes a way to describe the inertia tensor $\mathbf{\InertiaTensor}$ with respect to a different position. 

In case $\mathbf{\InertiaTensor}$ is described with respect to some frame $\mathcal{Q}$ with an origin at $\mathbf{p}_q$, it is possible to describe $\mathbf{\InertiaTensor}$ with respect to a different frame $\mathcal{P}$ with an origin at $\mathbf{p}_p$, provided that the axis of $\mathcal{Q}$ and $\mathcal{P}$ are parallel.

Let's define $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{p}_p - \mathbf{p}_q$. Then, 

\begin{equation}
	\begin{matrix}
    	I_{xx}^{\mathcal{P}} = I_{xx}^{\mathcal{Q}} + m \times (r_y^2 + r_z^2)  & I_{yy}^{\mathcal{P}} = I_{yy}^{\mathcal{Q}} + m \times (r_x^2 + r_z^2) & I_{zz}^{\mathcal{P}} = I_{zz}^{\mathcal{Q}} + m \times (r_x^2 + r_y^2) \\
    I_{xy}^{\mathcal{P}} = I_{xy}^{\mathcal{Q}} - m \times r_x \times r_y & I_{xz}^{\mathcal{P}} = I_{xz}^{\mathcal{Q}} - m \times r_x \times r_z & I_{yz}^{\mathcal{P}} = I_{yz}^{\mathcal{Q}} - m \times r_y \times r_z
	\end{matrix}
	\label{eq:parallelaxis}
\end{equation}

The set of equations in \ref{eq:parallelaxis} sum up the parallel-axis theorem analytically, where $m$ stands for the body mass.

\section{Linear algebra}
\label{section:linalgebra}

\begin{definition}[Symmetric matrix]
A matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is a symmetric in case $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}^T$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Matrix eigenvalues]
For some symmetric matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, we define $\mathbf{Q}(\mathbf{z}) := \{ \forall \mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^n: \mathbf{z}^T \mathbf{B} \mathbf{z} \}$ and the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{B}$ as $\text{eig}(\mathbf{B})$. Then, the following definitions are stated:

\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathbf{B}$ is positive semi-definite ($\mathbf{B} \succeq 0$) in case $\mathbf{Q}(\mathbf{z}) \geq 0 \Leftrightarrow \text{min}\left( \text{eig}(\mathbf{B}) \right) \geq 0$.\\
\item $\mathbf{B}$ is positive definite ($\mathbf{B} \succ 0$) in case $\mathbf{Q}(\mathbf{z}) > 0 \Leftrightarrow \text{min}\left( \text{eig}(\mathbf{B}) \right) > 0$.\\
\item $\mathbf{B}$ is negative semi-definite ($\mathbf{B} \preceq 0$) in case $\mathbf{Q}(\mathbf{z}) \leq 0 \Leftrightarrow \text{max}\left( \text{eig}(\mathbf{B}) \right) \leq 0$.\\
\item $\mathbf{B}$ is negative definite ($\mathbf{B} \prec 0$) in case $\mathbf{Q}(\mathbf{z}) < 0 \Leftrightarrow \text{max}\left( \text{eig}(\mathbf{B}) \right) < 0$.\\
\item $\mathbf{B}$ is indefinite in case $\text{min}\left( \text{eig}(\mathbf{B}) \right) < 0$ and $\text{max}\left( \text{eig}(\mathbf{B}) \right) > 0$.
\end{itemize}

, where $\text{min}\left( \text{eig}(\mathbf{B}) \right)$ and $\text{max}\left( \text{eig}(\mathbf{B}) \right)$ denote the minimum and maximum eigenvalues of $\mathbf{B}$, respectively.
\label{def:matrixeigenvalues}
\end{definition}
