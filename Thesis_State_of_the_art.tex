\section{Path planning algorithms}
\label{section:pathPlanningAlgorithms}

Firstly, this section presents a review on the modelling of outdoor, rough terrain maps based on \cite{chapter45handbookrobotics}. Then, a cost-map based optimal path planning literature review is presented. Finally, a detailed explanation of the fast marching method principles is introduced.

\subsection{World modelling}
\label{subsection:worldModelling}

World modelling falls into one of the following categories (figure \ref{fig:worldsMaps}): indoor and structured environments, natural terrains or dynamic environments. In this thesis, one is interested in performing a metrical evaluation of a highly irregular, outdoor environment with the abscence of dynamic obstacles, so a brief review of models for natural terrains is presented.

\label{fig:worldsMaps}

Elevation grids or \tb{digital elevation maps} (DEM) are geometric models which store the elevation $\z$ of a discrete location $(\x_i, \y_j)$. Elevation grids are generated due to sensor data. This retrieval of data may be aerial or on the ground. In case of aerial retrieved data, a regularly sampled grid is typically the end result, due to uniform sensor data. If data is retrieved from the ground, the quality of the data depends on the incidence angle and the distance from the sensor to the location $(\x_i, \y_j)$, which typically yields a variable-sized grid. In the work of \citet{fankhauser2014robot}, a robot-centric, online updated elevation map is estimated, where each cell stores a height estimate $\hat{\z}$ and a variance $\hat{\sigma}_z^2$. In \cite{Correal2011}, a robot-centered DEM is built from a 3D point cloud, which was generated by a pair of stereo cameras. Aerial data \cite{Smith2001} is also used to deliver an \ti{a-priori} global grid of the target environment, with an accuracy that can be improved by a ground, robot-centered mapping system \cite{Jayasekara2012, Correal2011}.  Additionally, information on how to tackle issues such as sensor resolution, uncertainty and terrain occlusion can be found in \cite{chapter45handbookrobotics}.

\tb{3-D grids and point sets} extend the previous approach to a 3D representation. Typically, this approach handle large sets of points where free or occupied areas cannot be properly defined. Octotrees are a solution to this problem. Then, the octomap approach \cite{Jayasekara2012} uses octotrees to support compact memory representation, multiresolution queries and probabilistic occupancy estimates. A great advantage is that it is possible to model areas which are occluded by vegetation and assign occupancy costs to them.

\tb{Meshes} present an improvement to digtal elevation maps and 3-D grids. It is a compact representation and presents continuity over the surface. However, this method requires simplifications techniques to lower computational costs and presents some difficulty when it comes to define complex environments as a continuous surface, such as vegetation. Therefore, precise data is needed so that discontinuities are detected.

\tb{Cost maps} are a feasible way to model traversability costs of a rough, uneven terrain and offer a safe and well-known approach to guidance systems on such kind of environments. \tb{Cost maps} may be updated \ti{in-situ} or generated \ti{prior} to placement with overhead imagery. Usually, the computation of costs takes into account the robot kinematics \cite{Amorim2014} and dynamics \cite{Yokokohji2007}, as well as terrain inclination. The definition of an explicit cost function is arbitrary, although the robot's limitations are a factor to include. At the end, the obtained traversability map can be combined with a minimum-cost planner \cite{Amorim2014, Ishigami2007PathPlanning}, in order to output optimal paths into a control system. In the work of \citet{Amorim2014}, a traversability cost is generated at each point by minimizing the height of a mobile robot at each location $(\x_i, \y_j)$. That minimization is carried out by fixing the center of mass of the robot at $(\x_i, \y_j)$, and then the mobile robot is lowered until the lowest admissable height is achieved. In the work of \citet{Ishigami2007PathPlanning}, a criteria function for the \tb{Dijkstra's} algorithm is described, by assigning to each cell a cost, that takes into account the terrain roughness around that cell, the distance to goal and terrain inclination. In \cite{Ventura2015}, a \tb{cost map} is created for an occupancy grid, by making obstacle space impossible to traverse and close to obstacle cells progressively less costly to traverse until a safety distance is reached.

\tb{Semantic attributes} (\tb{SA}) provide information about the type of terrain and objects found. This representation is called a \ti{high-level} one, in the sense that it groups data into broader classes of data, such as traversable or occupied regions, terrain type or vegetation. On the other hand, the previous approaches are defined as \ti{low-level}. Terrain classification methods \cite{Brooks2012self} are used to determine the type of terrain and landmark extraction is useful to detect saliencies in the environment.

Finally, \tb{heterogeneous and hierarchical models} (\tb{HHM}) offer hybrid maps which combine metric and feature maps. In \cite{Fankhauser2016GridMapLibrary}, a \verb!C++! library with Robot Operating System (ROS) and OpenCV interfaces is described. It manages a multi-layer map for rough terrain navigation. It is designed to store data such as elevation, traversability and surface normals.

\subsection{Literature review}
\label{subsection:pathPlanning} 

In rough terrain path planning literature, \textbf{artificial potential fields}, \textbf{optimal path planners} and \textbf{graph-search} algorithms arise as the main choices. An introductory review to rough terrain path planning is presented in \cite{Spero2002Path}.

\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Families of typical path planning methods on rough terrain.}
\label{fig:pathPlanningMethods}

In \cite{Ishigami2007PathPlanning}, the \textbf{Dijkstra's} algorithm is chosen due to its guaranteed convergence feature, but also because it allows for the optimization of a multiple-metric cost function. In \cite{Spero2002Path}, a \textbf{RRT} method is developped to apply a kinodynamic path planner to a 3D tessellated model. The work of \citet{sakayori2021energy} builds on top of \cite{Ishigami2007PathPlanning} and delivers a \textbf{Dijkstra's} based path planner that also takes into account the energy consumed by the rover when it moves between two nodes. Then, \cite{howard2007optimal} delivers an \textbf{optimal based} trajectory generation algorithm that aims to output a safe trajectory within a real-time window. This method includes the dynamics model of the rover into the NLP, compensates against wheel slippage and includes terrain uneveness into the method. 

\textbf{Artificial potential field} methods are also extensively used in rough terrain path planning. In the work of \citet{Amorim2014}, the \textbf{fast marching} method is employed in order to output an optimal path between two points. This method guarantees convergence as there are not local minimums. On the other hand, the goal point matches with the global minimum of the potential field, that helps generating the requested path from any given starting point. In \cite{garridoFMrovers2016}, more evidence is collected that ensures this method is capable of avoiding highly untraversable areas. Finally, a comparison between \textbf{RRT} and the \textbf{fast marching} method is laid out in \cite{garrido2009smooth}, where it is shown that the fast marching method outperforms RRT, not only on the path generation time, but also due to the fact that the fast marching method outputs a shorter path.

In this thesis, it was decided to develop a global path planning method on top of the work of \citet{Amorim2014}, as this implementation guarantees completeness, safety and feasibility for a real-time reference path update, within a real-time control algorithm.

\subsection{Fast marching method}
\label{section:fastMarching}

The fast marching method relies on solving a boundary-value problem \cite{Sethian1999}, which yields a potential field made up by a wave that starts at an \textit{a priori} defined source and expands throughout the domain of the map $\Map_2$. Mathematically, this comes to solving the Eikonal equation,
\begin{equation}
	\begin{cases}
		\left\vert \nabla \timeFunction{\x,\,\y} \right\vert \, \speedFunction{x,\, y} &= 1 \\
		\timeFunction{\NullTimePosition} &= 0
 	\end{cases}\hspace{5mm} , \hspace{2mm} \text{such that}\,(\x, \y) \in \Map_2\; \text{and} \; \NullTimePosition \subset \Map_2
	\label{eq:eikonalEquation}
\end{equation}
, where $\timeFunction{\cdot}$ is the solution to the Eikonal equation, $\mathbf{p}_2 = (\x, \y)$ is a position in the map and $\speedFunction{\cdot}$ is a speed (or cost) function \cite{Ventura2015} that contains information regarding the set of points where the wave delays or  rushes its progression throughout $\Map_{2}$. $\Gamma \subset \Map_{2}$ is the wave front at the source. Due to the fact that $u(\mathbf{p}_2)$ delivers the time a wave needs to reach $\mathbf{p}_2$ from the source $G \in \Map_{2}$, it is trivial that $u(\Gamma)$ equals zero because $\Gamma$ is a ball with neglegible radius around $G$. On the other hand, $u(\mathcal{P}) = T$ provides the set of points $\mathcal{P}$ from where it takes a time $T$ to reach $G$.

\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.45\textwidth}
  \begin{center}
  	\vspace{-15pt}
  	\includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{Figures/fm_example.png}
  \end{center}
  \caption{Example of global path generated by the fast marching method \cite{fm_marine_app}.}
  \label{fig:fmexample}
\end{wrapfigure}

Then, it is possible to find an optimal time path from $\mathbf{p}_2$ to $G$, which deviates from the points where $\speedFunction{\cdot}$ has the lowest values. The direction $-\nabla u(\mathbf{p}_2)$ of a path at some point is perpendicular to the wave front at that point. The function $\speedFunction{\cdot}$ is computed offline, and it is inserted into equation \eqref{eq:eikonalEquation}. After that, the Eikonal equation is solved (also offline) and delivers a potential field with a source at $G$. From there, it is possible to compute online the \textbf{safest} path until $G$ from the position the mobile robot is at. More information on this issue is provided at \cite{fmAdvancedGomez}.

\subsubsection*{Eikonal equation discretization}
\label{subsection:eikonalEqDiscretization}

\citet{fmAdvancedGomez} lays out an iterative algorithm for the computation of the discrete solution of equation \eqref{eq:eikonalEquation}. Here, a brief review of this algorithm is presented.

Equation \eqref{eq:eikonalEquation} may be discretized as,
\begin{equation}
	max\left( \frac{\timeFunction{\x_i, \y_j} - T_1}{\delta \x},\, 0 \right)^2 + max\left( \frac{\timeFunction{\x_i, \y_j} - T_2}{\delta \y},\, 0 \right)^2 = \frac{1}{\speedFunction{\x_i,\, \y_j}^2}
	\label{eq:eikonalDiscretization}
\end{equation}

\noindent , where
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		T_1 &= min\left( \timeFunction{\x_{i-1}, \y_j},\, \timeFunction{\x_{i+1}, \y_j} \right)\\
		T_2 &= min\left( \timeFunction{\x_i, \y_{j-1}},\, \timeFunction{\x_i, \y_{j+1}} \right)
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:t1t2}
\end{equation}

Then, equation \eqref{eq:eikonalDiscretization} is solved iteratively until every cell of the grid map is assigned a time to goal value, $\timeFunction{\x_i, \y_j}$. This algorithm starts solving \refeq{eq:eikonalDiscretization} at the neighbouring cells of the source cells $\NullTimePosition$, where $\timeFunction{\NullTimePosition} = 0$. As the algorithm progresses, it solves \refeq{eq:eikonalDiscretization} further away from $\NullTimePosition$, resembling the fact that the wave expands from the sources to each point of the gridmap. At the end, points which are further away from the sources, are typically assigned higher times, and no local minimums are present in the solution. \textbf{Algorithm 1 from \cite{fmAdvancedGomez}} details the implementation of this brief explanation.

\section{Optimal control literature review}
\label{section:ocpRoughTerrain}

In \cite{IagnemmaDubowsy2004} a comparison between a velocity-controlled and a rough terrain control system is presented. In this paper, an optimization is performed in order to achieve the desired wheel torques, that safeguard against terrain failure or accounts for power consumption depending on the terrain uneveness. The \textbf{wheel-terrain contact angles} are also estimated in order to include terrain irregularity into the model. At the end, the rough-terrain control system outperforms in terms of the average recorded slip and generally performs well on highly challenging terrain, because the rough terrain control system accounts for the dynamics of the robot, whereas the velocity-based control system does not. However, the authors only tested the algorithm on longitudinal paths and did not account for roll effects or change of directions.

With the same spirit, the work of \citet{bussmannPlanetaryRoverImpedanceControl} developped a cartesian impedance control that outputs some desired wrench to be followed. Then, an optimization problem is employed to properly distribute the wheel forces, in order to account for wheel-terrain constraints but also to meet the desired impedance wrench. The weight matrices were also adjusted based on whether power minimization was of interest or not, as saving power may increase the amount of returned science. The authors tested their implementation on a surface with different frictions, which resembles the fact that the surface may go from rocky to sandy. Generally, the method compensates against slip and tracks the desired wrench, although it can only optimize for one feature at a time.

In \cite{lbnmpcOstafew}, a \textbf{nonlinear model predictive control algorithm} is applied to a skid-steering robot that follows a closed path repeatedly. The kinematics is included in the model, but the dynamics is learned through a \textbf{gaussian process model}. The method achieves good tracking results, although it was not tested against high slopes. On the other hand, the works of \citet{binet2012model} and \citet{yu2022nmpc} present applications of NMPC to wheeled mobile robots, which are closer to what is intended to achieve in this thesis. In \cite{binet2012model}, a \textbf{cascaded NMPC} method outputs good tracking under a high-fidelity terramechanics dynamics model. It nicely avoids obstacles, although it was not tested under very irregular terrain. In \cite{yu2022nmpc}, the author proposed an integration of the dynamics model into the NMPC, although it assumed that the mobile robot operates on a plane when the state of the robot is forward integrated along the \textbf{control horizon}. Moreover, the \textbf{bicycle model} is chosen as the wheel-terrain interaction dynamics and wheel-terrain contact angles are not taken into account as well. In this thesis, one is looking to implement the \textbf{Coulomb friction model} as the wheel-terrain interaction dynamics.

Although \cite{rathod2021model} presents a NMPC application to a legged robot, it is closer to what it is intended to deliver in this thesis, even if only longitudinal trajectories were fed to the controller. The authors introduced the contact angles into the model and a complete 6-DOFs dynamics. The coulomb friction model was also considered in order to avoid slippage. The main goal of the paper was to show a control method that properly ensures mobility and safety, as the authors tracked the motion of the robot against the \textbf{zero moment point margin} and ensured that friction forces stay well inside the \textbf{friction cone}. \texttt{acados} \cite{verschueren2022acados} was used for implementing a fast NMPC solver, like one aims to do in this thesis.

\textbf{Racing} focused NMPC formulations \cite{liniger2015optimization, mpc_frenetserret} also provide important insights, as these approaches aim to minimize traversal time and safeguard safety, by encouraging progress along the path within the vehicle limitations. In this thesis, a \textbf{virtual speed} state is included inside or outside the model, that aims to maximize the vehicle's speed. On the other hand, the work of \citet{kloesernmpc} uses a constant virtual speed state outside of the solver to maximize progress along the track. \textbf{Obstacle avoidance} is ensured and real-time specs are met by using \texttt{acados} to implement the well-known \textbf{real-time iteration method} (see subsection \ref{subsection:nmpc}).

\section{Nonlinear programming}
\label{section:nonlinearopt}

A general NLP \cite{diehlnumericalocp} is stated as follows,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\underset{\mathbf{w}}{\text{minimize}} \hspace{1mm} &f\left(\mathbf{w}\right)\\
	\text{subject to} &
	\begin{cases}
		\eqCon{\mathbf{w}} = \mathbf{0}\\
		\ineqCon{\mathbf{w}} \leq \mathbf{0}
	\end{cases}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:gennlp}
\end{equation}

\noindent , where $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ are the optimization variables and $f:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is the objetive function. $\mathbf{g}:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^{n_g}$ and $\mathbf{h}:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^{n_h}$ denote the equality and inequality constraint functions, respectively. These functions are assumed to be twice continuously differentiable.

The solution $\mathbf{w}^*$ of \refeq{eq:gennlp} is a feasible point $\mathbf{w}^* \in \feasibleSet$ and (at least) a local minimizer of \refeq{eq:gennlp}. If the NLP is convex, $\mathbf{w}^*$ is global minimizer of \refeq{eq:gennlp}. Some point $\mathbf{w}$ is a local minimizer, in case it satisfies \acrlong{kkt} optimality conditions.

\begin{theorem}[KKT conditions]
\label{theorem:kkt}
If $\mathbf{w}^*$ is a local minimizer of \ref{eq:gennlp} and \acrshort{licq} holds at $\mathbf{w}^*$, then there exist so called Lagrange multiplier vectors $\bm{\gmultiplier} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_g}$ and $\bm{\hmultiplier} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_h}$ such that,
\begin{subequations} \label{eq:kkt}
\begin{align}
\nabla f\left(\mathbf{w}^*\right) + \left(\bm{\gmultiplier}^*\right)^T \nabla\eqCon{\mathbf{w}^*} + \left( \bm{\hmultiplier}^* \right)^T \nabla\ineqCon{\mathbf{w}^*} &= \mathbf{0} \label{subeq:lagrangegrad} \\
\eqCon{\mathbf{w}^*} &= \mathbf{0} \label{subeq:eqcon}\\
\ineqCon{\mathbf{w}^*} &\leq \mathbf{0} \label{subeq:compcond1}\\
\bm{\hmultiplier}^* &\geq \mathbf{0} \label{subeq:compcond2}\\
\hmultiplier_i^* \, \ineqCon{\mathbf{w}^*}_i &= 0, \hspace{4mm} i = 1,\,\ldots,n_h \label{subeq:compcond3}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
\end{theorem}

\acrshort{kkt} conditions are the first-order optimality conditions for constrained \acrshort{nlp}s. These conditions are necessary for a local minimizer, but are enough in case \ref{eq:gennlp} is a convex \acrshort{nlp}.

Equation \eqref{subeq:lagrangegrad} is equivalent to $\nabla_\mathbf{w} \lagrangian{\mathbf{w}^*,\,\bm{\gmultiplier}^*,\,\bm{\hmultiplier}^*}$ , where $\lagrangian{\cdot}$ is  the so-called \textit{Lagrangian Function}.

\begin{definition}[Lagrangian function.]
The Lagrangian function $\mathcal{L}: \mathbb{R}^{n + n_g + n_h} \to \mathbb{R}$ is assumed to be a twice continuously differentiable function, and it is defined by,
\begin{equation}
\lagrangian{\mathbf{w},\,\bm{\gmultiplier},\,\bm{\hmultiplier}} = f\left(\mathbf{w}\right) + \bm{\gmultiplier}^T \eqCon{\mathbf{w}} + \bm{\hmultiplier}^T \ineqCon{\mathbf{w}}
\label{eq:lagrangianfunc}
\end{equation} 
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
In case \refeq{eq:gennlp} turns into an unconstrained problem, $\lagrangian{\mathbf{w},\,\bm{\gmultiplier},\,\bm{\hmultiplier}} = f\left(\mathbf{w}\right)$ and \refeq{subeq:lagrangegrad} reduces to $\nabla f\left(\mathbf{w}^*\right) = \mathbf{0}$. In the absence of inequalities, $\lagrangian{\mathbf{w},\,\bm{\gmultiplier},\,\bm{\hmultiplier}} = f\left(\mathbf{w}\right) \, + \, \bm{\gmultiplier}^T \eqCon{\mathbf{w}}$ and $\nabla f\left(\mathbf{w}^*\right) \, + \, \left(\bm{\gmultiplier}^*\right)^T \nabla\eqCon{\mathbf{w}^*} = 0$.
\end{remark}
Then, problem \eqref{eq:gennlp} may resort to an \textbf{unconstrained}, \textbf{equality constrained} or \textbf{inequality constrained} problem, depending on the existence of constraint functions $\ineqCon{\cdot}$ and $\eqCon{\cdot}$, as it is outlined in figure \ref{fig:nlptypeprob}.

\centering

Any algorithm that aims to solve problem \eqref{eq:gennlp} must be looking for feasible points that satisfy the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker optimality conditions. In this thesis, one is mainly concerned with inequality constrained NLPs, so a short review on methods that solve this family of problems is presented below.

\subsection*{Inequality Constrained Optimization}
\label{subsection:ineqconopt}

Inequality constrained NLPs are mainly solved by two big families of methods: interior-point methods (IPOPT) or Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP). Active-set methods are also a variant, but these methods are not used throughout this thesis.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[sibling distance=40mm, level distance=12mm]
  \node {\textbf{Newton type optimization methods} to solve inequality constrained problems}
    child {node {\textbf{IPOPT}}}
    child {node {\textbf{SQP}}}
    child {node {Active-set methods}};
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Summary of newton type optimization methods to solve inequality constrained problems.}
\label{fig:methodssolveineqcon}
\end{figure}

These methods are based on Newton's iterative method to solve a nonlinear rootfinding problem. In this case, the method is run until a solution $\left(\mathbf{w}_k,\,\bm{\gmultiplier}_k,\,\bm{\hmultiplier}_k\right)$ properly satisfies the KKT conditions with some neglegible error. The methods outlined in figure \ref{fig:methodssolveineqcon} iteratively linearize problem \eqref{eq:gennlp}, but present differences when it comes to handle the complementarity conditions \eqref{subeq:compcond1}, \eqref{subeq:compcond2} and \eqref{subeq:compcond3}.

\begin{remark}
A Newton method aims to solve the nonlinear equation $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{w})=0$, by converging to an optimal solution $\mathbf{w}^*$ through multiple linearizations of $\mathbf{f}(\cdot)$. One gets closer to $\mathbf{w}^*$ by iteratively calling $\mathbf{w}_{k+1} \gets \mathbf{w}_k - \left(\nabla^{-1} f\left(\mathbf{w}_k\right)\right)^T \, f\left(\mathbf{w}_k\right)$, until $\mathbf{w}_k$ satisfies the KKT conditions with neglegible error. The numerical treatment of $\nabla^{-1} f\left(\mathbf{w}_k\right)$ is mentioned below, and gives rise to different variants of this method \cite{diehl2009efficient, diehlnumericalocp}.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection*{Sequential quadratic programming}
\label{subsubsection:sqp}

Sequential quadratic programming is based on linearizing all nonlinear functions from problem \eqref{eq:gennlp}. At the end, one gets a quadratic programming (QP) problem as follows,

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\underset{\mathbf{w}}{\text{minimize}} \hspace{1mm} &\nabla f\left(\mathbf{w}_k\right)^T (\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{w}_k) + \frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{w}_k)^T \mathbf{B}_k (\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{w}_k)\\
	\text{subject to} &
	\begin{cases}
		\eqCon{\mathbf{w}_k} + \nabla \eqCon{\mathbf{w}_k}^T (\mathbf{w}_k - \mathbf{w}) = 0\\
		\ineqCon{\mathbf{w}_k} + \nabla \ineqCon{\mathbf{w}_k}^T (\mathbf{w}_k - \mathbf{w}) \leq 0
	\end{cases}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:sqp}
\end{equation}

\noindent , where $\mathbf{B}_k$ may take the exact hessian $\nabla_\mathbf{w}^2 \lagrangian{\mathbf{w}_k,\,\bm{\gmultiplier}_k,\,\bm{\hmultiplier}_k}$ or inexact hessians and jacobians. More information on variants of SQP that take approximate Hessians and Jacobians, as well as respective convergence rates and code implementations can be found in \cite{diehl2009efficient, diehlnumericalocp}.

\begin{remark}
In case $\mathbf{B}_k = \nabla_\mathbf{w}^2 \lagrangian{\mathbf{w}_k,\,\bm{\gmultiplier}_k,\,\bm{\hmultiplier}_k}$ and this hessian is positive semi-definite, the QP is convex and a feasible point $\mathbf{w}^*$ is  global minimum of problem \eqref{eq:sqp}.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection*{\acrlong{ipopt}}
\label{subsubsection:ipopt}

On the other hand, interior-point methods aim to replace the complementarity conditions by a smooth nonlinear approximation $\hmultiplier_i^* \, h_i\left( \mathbf{w}^*\right) = \tau$, where $\tau > 0,\, i = 1,\,\ldots,n_h$. Then, the solution obtained from the Newton method concerns to the following problem,
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:ipopt}
	\begin{align}
		\underset{\mathbf{w}}{\text{minimize}} \hspace{1mm} &f\left(\mathbf{w}\right) - \tau \sum_{i=1}^{n_h} \log\left(-h_i\left( \mathbf{w}\right) \right) \label{subeq:ipoptobj}\\
		\text{subject to} \hspace{5mm} \eqCon{\mathbf{w}} &= 0\label{subeq:ipoptg}\\
		\hmultiplier_i^* \, h_i\left( \mathbf{w}^*\right) &= \tau \, | \,\tau > 0,\;i = 1,\,\ldots,n_h \label{subeq:ipoptprimaldual}
	\end{align}
\end{subequations}

Therefore, the solution to problem \eqref{eq:ipopt} is in the interior of the set described by inequalities \eqref{subeq:compcond1} and \eqref{subeq:compcond2}. Once a solution is found for a given $\tau$, this parameter can be reduced in order to find a closer solution to problem \eqref{eq:gennlp}, by iteratively using the previous solution as an initial guess to the next problem.

In fact, problem \eqref{eq:ipopt} is an equality constrained problem that is solved by iteratively applying the newton method to it. The literature reports good convergence to KKT points even for non-convex problems. In the case of convex problems, there's a link between the amount of newton iterations with respect to the required solution precision \cite{diehlnumericalocp}.

\begin{remark}
Most IPOPT solvers use a relaxed form of equation \eqref{subeq:ipoptprimaldual} by replacing it with a slacked formulation $h_i\left( \mathbf{w}^*\right) = s_i,\;i = 1,\,\ldots,n_h$. This formulation allows to provide an infeasible initial guess $\mathbf{w}$. It also avoids expensive backtracking of the newton method \cite{diehlnumericalocp}.
\end{remark}

\section{Optimal control problem}
\label{section:optimalControlProblems}

An optimal control problem is a particular case of a NLP. A continuous time optimal control problem \cite{diehlnumericalocp} is often defined as,

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{align}
		\underset{\mathbf{x}(\cdot),\,\mathbf{u}(\cdot)}{\text{minimize}} &\int_{t_0}^{t_f} \lagrangeTerm{\mathbf{x}(t),\, \mathbf{u}(t),\, \mathbf{p}(t)}\ dt + \mayerTerm{\mathbf{x}(t_f),\, \mathbf{p}(t_f)}\\
		\text{subject to}&
		\begin{cases}
			\mathbf{x}(t_0) =\mathbf{x}_0\\
			\DerMathBold{x}(t) = \processModel{\mathbf{x}(t),\,\mathbf{u}(t),\, \mathbf{p}(t),\,t} \quad & t \in [t_0, t_f]\\
			\ineqCon{\mathbf{x}(t),\,\mathbf{u}(t)} \leq 0\\
			\eqCon{\mathbf{x}(t),\,\mathbf{u}(t)} = 0
		\end{cases}
	\end{align}
	\label{eq:continuousTimeOCP}
\end{subequations}

\noindent , where $\mathbf{x}(t) \in \StateSpace$ stands for the state trajectory, $\mathbf{u}(t) \in \ControlSpace$ stands for the control trajectory and $\mathbf{p}(t)$ denotes the parameters of the problem. Function $\lagrangeTerm{\cdot}$ is the Lagrange function and $\mayerTerm{\cdot}$ is the Mayer term of the OCP cost. Similarly to problem \eqref{eq:gennlp}, $\processModel{\cdot}$ describes the model dynamics,  $\ineqCon{\cdot}$ stands for inequality constraints and $\eqCon{\cdot}$ contains equality constraints.

In order to solve problem \eqref{eq:continuousTimeOCP}, the problem needs to be reformulated numerically. Two numerical operations must be performed: \textbf{discretization} and \textbf{linearization}. These operations may be performed by \textbf{first linearizing and then discretizing} or by \textbf{first discretizing and then linearizing} \cite{gros2020linear}.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[sibling distance=60mm, level distance=12mm]
  \node {Numerical approaches}
    child {node {First linearise then discretize}}
    child {node {\textbf{First discretize then linearise}}};
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Summary of numerical approaches to problem \eqref{eq:continuousTimeOCP}.}
\label{fig:summarynumapproaches}
\end{figure}

In this thesis, the \textbf{first discretize then linearise} approach is chosen. This is due to the fact that the \textit{first linearise then discretize} approach performs the linearisation offline. This a limitation in case the reference is variable, because the linearisation becomes inexact \cite{gros2020linear}.

\subsection{Discretization methods of an optimal control problem}
\label{subsection:ocpdiscretization}

The literature provides the \textbf{Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation (HJB)} , \textbf{indirect methods} and \textbf{direct methods} as state-of-the art approaches to this issue (figure \ref{fig:summarydiscretizationapproaches}).

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[sibling distance=40mm, level distance=12mm]
  \node {Discretization methods}
    child {node {HJB}}
    child {node {Indirect methods}}
    child {node {\textbf{Direct methods}}
      child {node {Direct single shooting}}
      child {node {\textbf{Direct multiple shooting}}}
      child {node {Direct collocation}}
    };
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Summary of newton type methods to solve inequality constrained NLPs.}
\label{fig:summarydiscretizationapproaches}
\end{figure}

The HJB equation is a state-space approach that implies that each subarc of an optimal trajectory must be optimal. The \acrlong{hjb} is a partial differential equation that tracks the evolution of the cost function over time.

Indirect methods first optimize the problem and then discretize it. These methods derive a \textbf{boundary value problem} from the necessary conditions of optimality of the infinite dimensional problem. However, these methods present unstability and strong nonlinearities, and often require a new problem setup in case the control structure changes.

On the other hand, direct methods first discretize and then optimize. This approach can easily take care of all sorts of constraints. The control trajectory is parameterized along the control horizon, but the the state evolution is handled differently by each direct method variant. These methods are by far the most developed numerical approaches to an OCP. Three main direct methods are presented below:

\begin{itemize}[label=\raisebox{0.5ex}{\scalebox{0.4}{$\blacksquare$}}]
\item \textbf{Direct single shooting} is a sequential approach that only parameterizes controls in the NLP. Then, states are obtained by forward integration of $\processModel{\cdot}$ and constraints are also checked along the control horizon, with the parameterized controls as inputs. In this method, the controls are the only optimization variable.
\item \textbf{Direct multiple shooting} is a simultaneous approach that parameterizes controls and states in the NLP. First, it discretizes the control inputs, and then links the integration of $\processModel{\cdot}$ between each step of the control horizon. Each constraint is also checked along the control horizon. The lagrange hessian and the equality constraints jacobian are much sparser than the ones obtained with direct single shooting. This makes direct multiple shooting problems easier to solve, unless the control is extremely simple or the initial guess is very close to the solution \cite{Silva2022msc}.
\item \textbf{Direct collocation} is a simultaneous approach that discretizes both controls and states. On each interval of the control horizon, the trajectory of the state is approximated by a polynomial, and the controls are constant on that same interval. As it is stated on the direct multiple shooting transcription, continuity between states of consecutive time intervals is also required. This approach yields sparser jacobians and hessians as shooting methods, although collocation methods are typically used when the solution must be computed accurately \cite{Silva2022msc}.
\end{itemize}

As it is mentioned above, direct methods may be inserted into the sequential or simultaneous approach category. \textbf{Sequential approaches} first simulate and then optimize the system at hand. A \textbf{simultaneous approach} performs both tasks at the same time. A sequential approach has less variables, but doesn't preserve the problem's structure on the linear subproblems. On the other hand, a simultaneous approach presents faster local convergence rates, specially on highly nonlinear systems, because the nonlinearity is equally distributed along the control horizon \cite{diehl2009efficient}.

In this thesis, \texttt{acados} was selected as the test-bed software to solve OCPs. Direct multiple shooting is a built-in transcription method, because \texttt{acados} implements it to generate fast solvers. On the other hand, casADi is an alternative to implement an OCP, but this library is of a more general use and less focused on meeting real-time specs. However, casADi still remains a valid option in case one wants to transcribe an OCP using collocation methods and solve it with IPOPT \cite{wachter2006implementation}.

\subsubsection*{Direct multiple shooting transcription}
\label{subsubsection:dmstranscription}

According to the description provided above and on the work of \citet{bock2007numerical}, the NLP \eqref{eq:ocptranscription2dms} outlines a transcription of problem \eqref{eq:continuousTimeOCP} using direct multiple shooting.
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:ocptranscription2dms}
	\begin{align}
		\underset{\mathbf{w}}{\text{minimize}} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} \lagrangeTerm{\mathbf{x}(t_i),\,\mathbf{u}(t_i),\,&\mathbf{p}(t_i)}_i + \mayerTerm{\mathbf{x}(t_N),\,\mathbf{p}(t_N)} \label{subeq:objective} \\
		\text{subject to} \hspace{5mm} \mathbf{x}(t_0) &=\mathbf{\hat{x}}_0 \label{subeq:initialstate} \\
		\mathbf{x}(t_{i+1}) &= \processModel{\mathbf{x}(t_i),\,\mathbf{u}(t_i),\,\mathbf{p}(t_i)}_{RK4},\hspace{0.2cm} i= 0,\,...\,,N-1. \label{subeq:continuity} \\
		\ineqCon{\mathbf{x}(t_i),\,\mathbf{u}(t_i)} &\leq 0, \hspace{0.2cm} i= 0,\,...\,,N.  \label{subeq:nonlinearcons} \\
		\eqCon{\mathbf{x}(t_i),\,\mathbf{u}(t_i)} &= 0, \hspace{0.2cm} i= 0,\,...\,,N. \label{subeq:linearcons}
	\end{align}
\end{subequations}

\noindent , where equation \eqref{subeq:objective} denotes the objetive function, \eqref{subeq:initialstate} is the initial state constraint, \eqref{subeq:continuity} denotes the continuity constraint and \eqref{subeq:nonlinearcons} and \eqref{subeq:linearcons} denote the inequality and equality constraints, respectively. In this transcription, the optimization variables $\mathbf{w}$ from \refeq{eq:optimizationVar} include every state and control along the control horizon of length $N$. Every constraint is enforced at each subinterval $\delta t_i = \left[t_i,\,t_{i+1}\right]$, which may not be of equal time length.
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{w} = \left[\mathbf{x}^T(t_0),\,\mathbf{u}^T(t_0),\,\mathbf{x}^T(t_1),\,\mathbf{u}^T(t_1),\,\ldots,\mathbf{u}^T(t_{N-1}),\,\mathbf{x}^T(t_N) \right]
\label{eq:optimizationVar}
\end{equation}

Moreover, the dynamics of \refeq{subeq:continuity} is integrated using the very well known Runge-Kutta 4 method (see section \ref{section:rk4}).

\subsection{Linearisation after discretization}
\label{subsection:linafterdisc} 

When it comes to linearisation, subequation \eqref{subeq:continuity} is linearised by forward propagating the RK-4 integration method. For the sake of completeness, \textbf{implicit} and \textbf{explicit} integration methods are two classes of integrator methods available, whereas RK-4 is an explicit one. Implicit integrator schemes define a nonlinear system of equations that implicitly define the integration result. Then, this system is iteratively solved, similarly to a Newton type iterative method. On the other hand, explicit schemes rely on a direct combination of explicit evaluations of the right side of the system dynamics \cite{verschueren2022acados}.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[sibling distance=60mm, level distance=12mm]
  \node {Classes of integrators}
    child {node {Implicit schemes}}
    child {node {\textbf{Explicit schemes}}};
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Types of integrator schemes.}
\label{fig:integratormethods}
\end{figure}

The respective sensitivities are also differentiated. Here, the linearisation algorithm \nameref{alg:rk4ad} is presented, where $\mathbf{x}(t_k) = \mathbf{x}_k$ for the sake of simplicity. More details can be found in \cite{gros2020linear}. The propagation of $\odv{\mathbf{k}_j}{\mathbf{s}_i},\; \text{where} \, j = \{1,\,\ldots,4\},\; \mathbf{s}=\left[\mathbf{x}^T,\,\mathbf{u}^T\right]$ is derived by applying the chain rule to the propagation of $\mathbf{k}_j$. This algorithm is applied to every shooting node of problem \eqref{eq:ocptranscription2dms}. On every node, $N_s = \frac{\delta t_i}{h_i}$ integration steps are applied, with step $h_i$ \cite{gros2020linear}.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
	\caption{\textsc{RK4fAD}}\label{alg:rk4ad}
	\centering
	\footnotesize
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
	\Require $\mathbf{x}_i,\,\mathbf{u}_i$
	\State $\mathbf{A}_i = \mathbf{1},\,\mathbf{B}_i = \mathbf{0}$
	\ForEach{$j \in \{1,\, \ldots, \, N_s\}$}
	\State $\mathbf{k}_1 \gets \processModel{\mathbf{x}_i,\, \mathbf{u}_i,\, \mathbf{p}_i}$
	\State $\left[\odv{\mathbf{k}_1}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \odv{\mathbf{k}_1}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] \gets \pdv{\processModel{\mathbf{x}_i,\, \mathbf{u}_i,\, \mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{x}_i} \left[\mathbf{A}_i \hspace{0.2cm} \mathbf{B}_i\right] + \left[\mathbf{0} \hspace{0.2cm} \pdv{\mathbf{f}}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right]$
	\State $\mathbf{k}_2 \gets \processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + \frac{h_i}{2}\mathbf{k}_1,\,\mathbf{u}_i,\,\mathbf{p}_i}$
	\State $\left[\odv{\mathbf{k}_2}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \odv{\mathbf{k}_2}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] \gets \pdv{\processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + \frac{h_i}{2}\mathbf{k}_1,\,\mathbf{u}_i,\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{x}_i} \left[\mathbf{A}_i + \frac{h_i}{2} \odv{\mathbf{k}_1}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \mathbf{B}_i + \frac{h_i}{2} \odv{\mathbf{k}_1}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] + \left[\mathbf{0} \hspace{0.2cm} \pdv{\processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + \frac{h_i}{2}\mathbf{k}_1,\,\mathbf{u}_i,\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right]$
	\State $\mathbf{k}_3 \gets \processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + \frac{h_i}{2}\mathbf{k}_2,\, \mathbf{u}_i,\, \mathbf{p}_i}$
	\State $\left[\odv{\mathbf{k}_3}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \odv{\mathbf{k}_3}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] \gets \pdv{\processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + \frac{h_i}{2}\mathbf{k}_2,\,\mathbf{u}_i,\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{x}_i} \left[\mathbf{A}_i + \frac{h_i}{2} \odv{\mathbf{k}_2}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \mathbf{B}_i + \frac{h_i}{2} \odv{\mathbf{k}_2}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] + \left[\mathbf{0} \hspace{0.2cm} \pdv{\processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + \frac{h_i}{2}\mathbf{k}_2,\,\mathbf{u}_i,\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right]$
	\State $\mathbf{k}_4 \gets \processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + h_i\,\mathbf{k}_3,\, \mathbf{u}_i,\, \mathbf{p}_i}$
	\State $\left[\odv{\mathbf{k}_4}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \odv{\mathbf{k}_4}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] \gets \pdv{\processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + h_i \, \mathbf{k}_3,\, \mathbf{u}_i,\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{x}_i} \left[\mathbf{A}_i + h_i \, \odv{\mathbf{k}_3}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \mathbf{B}_i + h_i \, \odv{\mathbf{k}_3}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] + \left[\mathbf{0} \hspace{0.2cm} \pdv{\processModel{\mathbf{x}_i + h_i \, \mathbf{k}_3,\,\mathbf{u}_i,\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right]$
	\State $\mathbf{x}_i \gets \mathbf{x}_i + \frac{h_i}{6} \left( \mathbf{k}_1 + 2 \, \mathbf{k}_2 + 2 \, \mathbf{k}_3 + \mathbf{k}_4 \right)$
	\State $\left[\mathbf{A}_i \hspace{0.2cm} \mathbf{B}_i\right] \gets \left[\mathbf{A}_i \hspace{0.2cm} \mathbf{B}_i\right] + \frac{h_i}{6} \, \left( \left[\odv{\mathbf{k}_1}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \odv{\mathbf{k}_1}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] + 2 \, \left[\odv{\mathbf{k}_2}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \odv{\mathbf{k}_2}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] + 2 \, \left[\odv{\mathbf{k}_3}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \odv{\mathbf{k}_3}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] + \left[\odv{\mathbf{k}_4}{\mathbf{x}_i} \hspace{0.2cm} \odv{\mathbf{k}_4}{\mathbf{u}_i}\right] \right)$
	\EndFor
	\State $\mathbf{x}_{i+1} \gets \mathbf{x}_i,\;\mathbf{A}_{i+1} \gets \mathbf{A}_i,\;\mathbf{B}_{i+1} \gets \mathbf{B}_i$
	\State \Return $\mathbf{x}_{i+1},\,\mathbf{A}_{i+1},\,\mathbf{B}_{i+1}$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

In algorithm \nameref{alg:rk4ad}, $\mathbf{A}_i = \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}_{RK4}}{\mathbf{x}_i}$ and $\mathbf{B}_i = \pdv{\processModel{\cdot}_{RK4}}{\mathbf{u}_i}$, where $\processModel{\cdot}_{RK4}$ denotes $\processModel{\mathbf{x}(t_i),\,\mathbf{u}(t_i),\,\mathbf{p}(t_i)}_{RK4}$, for the sake of simplicity. For the sake of completeness, first-order sensitivities are presented in \refeq{eq:sensitivitiespropagation}. Additionally, the reader is referred to \cite{verschueren2022acados}, to collect information on second-order sensitivities.

\subsubsection*{Quadratic programming}

As a result of the application of these numerical methods to problem \eqref{eq:ocptranscription2dms}, one gets QP \eqref{eq:qpocp}. A slacked formulation was omitted, but the reader may find it in \cite{verschueren2022acados}. Slack variables relax the problem constraints and can be introduced in the objective function. Apart from that, these variables are also part of the optimization variables $\Delta \mathbf{w}$.

However, for the case of QP \eqref{eq:qpocp}, $\Delta \mathbf{w}$ only includes the states and control inputs. Moreover, $\mathbf{H}_i$ denotes the hessian of the lagrangian of problem \eqref{eq:ocptranscription2dms} and $\left[\mathbf{q}^T_i \; \mathbf{r}^T_i\right]$ stands for the lagrangian gradient linear terms, which are defined on equations \eqref{subeq:qphessian} and \eqref{subeq:hessian2}, respectively.

For the sake of brevity, approaches to address the approximation of the hessian $\mathbf{H}_i$ are divided in two families: \textbf{approximation of convex hessians} or \textbf{regularization} of indefinite hessians. These methods aim to improve convergence of the NMPC solver scheme and the hessian convexity ($\mathbf{H}_i \succeq 0$).
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:qpdefinitions}
\begin{align}
\mathbf{H}_i &= \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{Q}_i & \mathbf{S}_i^T\\
\mathbf{S}_i & \mathbf{R}_i
\end{bmatrix},\; i = 0,\,\ldots,N-1 \label{subeq:qphessian} \\
\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{q}_i \\ \mathbf{r}_i \end{bmatrix} &= \mathbf{W}_i \, \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_i^{guess} -  \mathbf{x}_i^{ref} \\  \mathbf{u}_i^{guess} -  \mathbf{u}_i^{ref} \end{bmatrix},\; i = 0,\,\ldots,N-1 \label{subeq:hessian2} \\
\Delta \mathbf{w} &= \left[\Delta \mathbf{x}^T_0, \,\Delta \mathbf{u}^T_0, \, \Delta \mathbf{x}^T_1, \,\Delta \mathbf{u}^T_1,\,\ldots, \Delta \mathbf{u}^T_{N-1},\, \Delta \mathbf{x}^T_N \right]  \label{subeq:qpoptvar} \\
\Delta \mathbf{x}_i &= \mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_i^{guess},\; i = 0,\,\ldots,N-1 \label{subeq:qpstates} \\
\Delta \mathbf{u}_i &= \mathbf{u}_i - \mathbf{u}_i^{guess},\; i = 0,\,\ldots,N-1\label{subeq:qpcontrols}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

Moreover, QP \eqref{eq:qpocp} can be solved by further exploiting the QP structure. The \textbf{condensing approach} is a very popular one that creates a smaller QP by eliminating the state variables. Then, a dense QP solver such as \texttt{qpOASES} \cite{ferreau2014qpoases} or an IPOPT method like \texttt{HPIPM} \cite{frison2020hpipm} can be applied to this smaller QP. Both are available in \texttt{acados}. \textbf{Partial condensing} methods only create smaller QPs on some blocks of the control horizon, although it keeps some of the initial QP structure. The reader is referred to \cite{verschueren2022acados, diehl2009efficient} for more information on the hessian approximation topic and on QP structure exploitation.
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:qpocp}
	\begin{align}
		\underset{\Delta \mathbf{w}}{\text{minimize}} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} & \frac{1}{2} \, \begin{bmatrix}
		\Delta \mathbf{x}_i\\
		\Delta \mathbf{u}_i
\end{bmatrix}^T \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{Q}_i & \mathbf{S}_i^T\\
\mathbf{S}_i & \mathbf{R}_i
\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
		\Delta \mathbf{x}_i\\
		\Delta \mathbf{u}_i
\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}
		\mathbf{q}_i\\
		\mathbf{r}_i
\end{bmatrix}^T \begin{bmatrix}
		\Delta \mathbf{x}_i\\
		\Delta \mathbf{u}_i
\end{bmatrix} + \Delta \mathbf{x}_N^T \, \mathbf{Q}_N \, \Delta \mathbf{x}_N + \mathbf{q}_N^T \, \Delta \mathbf{x}_N \label{subeq:qpobjective}\\
		\text{subject to} \hspace{5mm} \Delta \mathbf{x}_0 &= \mathbf{\hat{x}}_0 - \mathbf{x}_0^{guess} \label{subeq:qpinitialstate}\\
		\Delta \mathbf{x}_{i+1} &= \mathbf{A}_i \, \Delta \mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{B}_i \, \Delta \mathbf{u}_i + \processModel{\mathbf{x}_i^{guess},\, \mathbf{u}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_i}_{RK4} - \mathbf{x}_{i+1}^{guess},\, \hspace{0.1cm} i= 0,\,...\,,N-1. \label{subeq:qpcontinuity}\\
		0 &\geq \mathbf{C}_i\,\Delta \mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{D}_i \,\,\Delta \mathbf{u}_i + \ineqCon{\mathbf{x}_i^{guess},\, \mathbf{u}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_i}, \hspace{0.2cm} i= 0,\,...\,,N-1 \label{subeq:qpnonlinearcons}\\
		0 &= \mathbf{E}_i \, \Delta \mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{F}_i \, \Delta \mathbf{u}_i + \eqCon{\mathbf{x}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{u}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_i}, \hspace{0.2cm} i= 0,\,...\,,N-1 \label{subeq:qplinearcons}\\
		0 &\geq \mathbf{C}_N \, \Delta \mathbf{x}_N + \ineqCon{\mathbf{x}_N^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_N}\\
		0 &= \mathbf{E}_N \, \Delta \mathbf{x}_N + \eqCon{\mathbf{x}_N^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_N}
	\end{align}
\end{subequations}

\noindent , where the Jacobian matrices of $\ineqCon{\cdot}$ and $\eqCon{\cdot}$ are denoted by $\mathbf{C}_i = \pdv{\ineqCon{\mathbf{x}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{u}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{x}_i}$, $\mathbf{D}_i = \pdv{\ineqCon{\mathbf{x}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{u}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{u}_i}$, $\mathbf{E}_i = \pdv{\eqCon{\mathbf{x}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{u}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{x}_i}$, $\mathbf{F}_i = \pdv{\eqCon{\mathbf{x}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{u}_i^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_i}}{\mathbf{u}_i}$.

Finally, it is worth noting that the sum $\processModel{\mathbf{x}_i^{guess}, \mathbf{u}_i^{guess}, \mathbf{p}_i}_{RK4} - \mathbf{x}_{i+1}^{guess}$ denotes an offset term that expresses whether the guess satisfies the problem dynamics. In case this term is zero, the QP solver focuses on rejecting disturbances and on minimizing the objective function. 

\subsection{Nonlinear Model Predictive Control}
\label{subsection:nmpc}

A \acrshort{nmpc} problem \cite{Rawlings2013, grunenmpc} lies in optimizing a cost function, which may or not be subject to constraints. The cost function is optimized along an established horizon (it may be infinite theoretically), and the constraints may range from model-based dynamics to state or control constraints. This problem is also subject to initial values. A NMPC formulation example is provided by problem \eqref{eq:ocptranscription2dms}, whereas an example of it is presented in \eqref{eq:safeonestageocp}. Figure \ref{fig:mpcusecase}\footnote{\textit{A basic working principle of Model Predictive Control} (October 2009) from  Martin Behrendt: \url{https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:MPC_scheme_basic.svg} (accessed on 26 of March 2026)} presents a basic working example.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/MPC_scheme_basic.pdf}
  \caption[Working principle of a discrete OCP.]{NMPC working principle.}
  \label{fig:mpcusecase}
\end{figure}

Typically, this problem is formulated on closed loop form, and computes a control law $\pi(\cdot)$, that delivers an optimized control $\mathbf{u}_0^*$ to be applied to a plant, such that $\mathbf{u}_0^* = \pi\left(\mathbf{\hat{x}}_0\right)$. In this thesis, only SQP methods are considered to solve a NMPC problem. Typically, such problems iteratively solve QPs like the one in \eqref{eq:qpocp} by providing Newton directions $\Delta \mathbf{w}_k$ to update the solution $\mathbf{w}_{k+1}$, apart from the Lagrange multipliers $\bm{\hmultiplier}_k$ and $\bm{\gmultiplier}_k$ (\ref{eq:updatesol}), until some convergence criteria is met (see algorithms 1 and 2 of \cite{gros2020linear}).

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{w}_k = \mathbf{w}_k^{guess} + \Delta \mathbf{w}_k ,\hspace{0.5cm} \bm{\hmultiplier}_k = \bm{\hmultiplier}_{QP}, \hspace{0.5cm} \bm{\gmultiplier}_k = \bm{\gmultiplier}_{QP}
\label{eq:updatesol}
\end{equation}

A survey on online NMPC optimization algorithms is presented in the final chapter of \cite{diehl2009efficient}. This thesis focuses on the application of the real-time iteration scheme. The work of \citet{bock2007numerical} presents variants of this scheme. Below, one describes the RTI algorithm as it is implemented in \texttt{acados}.

\subsubsection*{Real-time iteration scheme}

RTI aims to tackle shortcomings of the NMPC SQP solver. Although the latter provides more accurate solutions, it delivers outdated control policies. At the moment that the solution is provided, there is a delay between the start of the algorithm (when the initial state is gathered) and its end (\textbf{real-time dilemma}). Due to that, the \acrshort{rti} scheme compromises accuracy but scores on delivering a control policy that is based on up to date information regarding the physical system state.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
  \centering
  \begin{subfigmatrix}{4}
  	\subfigure[On the SQP algorithm, the state measurement $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_k$ used to compute $\mathbf{u}^*_k$ becomes outdated, as significant time has passed between the state gathering and plant actuation.]{\includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{Figures/sqp_time.pdf}}
    \subfigure[On the \nameref{alg:rti} scheme, the time between the state gathering and the plant actuation is significantly reduced, as the preparation phase takes care in advance of numerical operations independent of the plant state.]{\includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{Figures/rti.pdf}}
  \end{subfigmatrix}
  \caption{Comparison between SQP and \nameref{alg:rti} algorithms \cite{gros2020linear}.}
  \label{fig:contactModels}
\end{figure}

The algorithm is divided between a \textbf{preparation} - it performs all computations that do not require the knowledge of the system current state $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_0$ - and a \textbf{feedback} - it fastly obtains a new Newton direction $\Delta \mathbf{w}$ after obtaining $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_0$ - phase. Algorithm \nameref{alg:rti} joins these two phases, where $i$ denotes the control horizon node and $k$ denotes the $k^{th}$ QP iteration.

\begin{remark}
In case $\mathbf{H}_{i, k} = \mathbf{W}_{i, k}$, it comes that $\mathbf{H}_{i, k} \succeq 0$, and there is no need to compute second-order sensitivities. Note that a non-positive definite cost does not ensure stability of the algorithm \cite{gros2020linear}.
\end{remark}

Due to the nature of the \nameref{alg:rti} scheme, it only performs a full Newton step per iteration. On the other hand, the SQP can perform multiple steps per iteration until convergence is achieved. Then, some measures must be put in place in order to ensure that the solution provided by this single step is close enough to a fully converged and global optimal solution. 

\subsubsection*{\acrshort{rti} global optimality}

Under some assumptions \cite{gros2020linear}, the solution provided by the \acrshort{rti} scheme is considered to be on the neighborhood of the \acrshort{nmpc} problem global solution, such that $\mathbf{w}_{RTI}^* \in \neighborhood\left( \mathbf{w}_{NMPC}^* \right)$. These are:

\begin{assumption}\label{assumption:rti1}
The \acrshort{rti} scheme is warm-started at the global optimum.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{assumption:rti2}
The sampling frequency $\frac{1}{\delta t_i}$ is sufficiently high.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{assumption:rti3}
There are no jumps in the reference and state.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{assumption:rti4}
The \acrshort{ocp} underlying the \acrshort{nmpc} problem fullfils the second order sufficient conditions (see theorem \ref{theorem:sooc}) for every feasible initial condition.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{assumption:rti5}
The global optimum depends continuously on the initial state and reference.
\end{assumption}

Assumption \ref{assumption:rti1} entails the fact that the initial guess must be close to the global optimum. If we assume that the previous solution verifies the latter requirement, that is $\mathbf{w}_{k-1} \in \neighborhood\left( \mathbf{w}_{NMPC}^* \right)$, then $\mathbf{w}_{k-1}$ may be shifted to the following iteration and warm-up \ref{eq:qpocp} with the previous solution. Assumptions \ref{assumption:rti2} and \ref{assumption:rti3} contribute to guarantee the previous proposition along every RTI iteration. Assumption \ref{assumption:rti4} guarantees that the solution manifold $\neighborhood\left(\mathbf{w}_{NMPC}^*\right)$ is smooth and has no bifurcation. Then, algorithm \nameref{alg:rti} \cite{gros2020linear} will keep track of $\neighborhood\left( \mathbf{w}_{NMPC}^* \right)$ as long as $\mathbf{w}_{k}^{guess} \in \neighborhood\left( \mathbf{w}_{NMPC}^* \right)$ and $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_{0, k} \in \neighborhood\left( \mathbf{\hat{x}}_{0, k}^{guess}\right)$. Finally, assumption \ref{assumption:rti5} guarantees that the solution manifold $\neighborhood\left( \mathbf{w}_{NMPC}^* \right)$ is continuous in time. In case these assumptions are taken into account, the application of the \nameref{alg:rti} algorithm at iteration $k$ can be said to deliver the global optimum of the \acrshort{nmpc} problem, and not just a local one. See the work of \citet{diehl2001real} for a formal proof of this statement.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
	\caption{\textsc{RTI}}\label{alg:rti}
	\centering
	\footnotesize
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
	\Statex \underline{Preparation phase} performed over the interval $\left[t_{k-1},\,t_k\right]$
	\Require previous solution $\mathbf{w}_{k-1}$ and references $\mathbf{x}_k^{ref},\,\mathbf{u}_k^{ref}$
	\State Shift $\mathbf{w}_{k-1}$ to construct $\mathbf{w}_k^{guess}$
	\State Evaluate $\processModel{\cdot},\, \eqCon{\cdot},\,\ineqCon{\cdot},\,\mathbf{A}_{i,\,k},\,\mathbf{B}_{i,\,k},\,\mathbf{C}_{i,\,k},\,\mathbf{D}_{i,\,k},\,\mathbf{E}_{i,\,k},\,\mathbf{F}_{i,\,k},\,\mathbf{H}_{i,\,k},\,\mathbf{W}_{i,\,k}$
	\State Build QP \eqref{eq:qpocp} without the knowledge of $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_{0, k}$ and prepare all possible computations (hessian approximation, QP condensing, etc)
	\State \Return \eqref{eq:qpocp}
	\Statex \underline{Feedback phase} performed at time $t_k$ upon availability of $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_{0, k}$
	\Require Initial state $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_{0, k}$ and previously prepared QP \eqref{eq:qpocp}
	\State Compute Newton direction update $\Delta \mathbf{w}_k$ by introducing $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_{0, k}$ into \eqref{eq:qpocp} and solving it
	\State Apply the full Newton step
	\Statex \centerline{$\mathbf{w}_k \gets \mathbf{w}_{k}^{guess} + \Delta \mathbf{w}_{k},\hspace{0.3cm}\bm{\gmultiplier}_k \gets \bm{\gmultiplier}_{QP},\hspace{0.3cm}\bm{\hmultiplier}_k \gets \bm{\hmultiplier}_{QP}$}
	\State \Return $\mathbf{w}_k$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection*{Initial guess shifting}

In order to meet assumption \ref{assumption:rti1}, a good shifting of $\mathbf{w}_{k-1}$ is needed to place $\mathbf{w}_k^{guess}$, sufficiently close to the global optimum solution. This process shifts each element  of $\mathbf{w}_{k-1}$ to the previous node, as follows.
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{x}_{i,\,k}^{guess} &= \mathbf{x}_{i+1,\,k-1}, \hspace{0.3cm} i = 0,\,\ldots,\,N-1\\
\mathbf{u}_{i,\,k}^{guess} &= \mathbf{u}_{i+1,\,k-1}, \hspace{0.3cm} i = 0,\,\ldots,\,N-2\\
\mathbf{x}_{N,\,k}^{guess} &= \processModel{\mathbf{x}_{N-1,\,k}^{guess},\,\mathbf{u}_{N-1,\,k}^{guess},\,\mathbf{p}_N}_{RK4}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:shifting}
\end{equation}

Then, several approaches may be followed to generate the last control $\mathbf{u}_{N-1,\,k}^{guess}$ of the next guess \cite{gros2020linear}. Here, the simplest one is presented that copies $\mathbf{u}_{N-2,\,k}^{guess}$ from the third-to-last node to the next-to-last node, that is $\mathbf{u}_{N-1,\,k}^{guess} = \mathbf{u}_{N-2,\,k}^{guess}$.
